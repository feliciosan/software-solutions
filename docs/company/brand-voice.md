## Why this document exists

Voice is what makes our content recognizably ours across the blog, LinkedIn, and every surface. This is the reference for tone, style, vocabulary, and formatting, so anything we publish sounds like the same team of engineers.

---

## 1. Who we sound like

A senior engineer explaining something to a respected peer over coffee: **direct, knowledgeable, honest, and generous.** Confident without arrogance, technical without gatekeeping, opinionated but backed by evidence.

We write **engineer to engineer.** We assume the reader is competent and busy.

---

## 2. Tone

- **Direct.** Lead with the point. Cut throat-clearing.
- **Practical.** Everything ties back to doing real work better.
- **Opinionated, with evidence.** Take positions; back them with code, benchmarks, or experience. Say when something is preference vs fact.
- **Honest.** Name trade-offs and limits, including what does not work.
- **Warm and human.** Technical does not mean cold. A little personality is welcome.
- **Calm.** No hype, no fear, no clickbait.

---

## 3. Writing style

- **Second person and active voice.** "You configure…", not "one might configure…".
- **Short paragraphs, clear structure.** Descriptive headings, scannable sections.
- **Show, do not just tell.** Real, runnable code and concrete examples.
- **Precise.** Correct terminology, version-aware, specific ("Next.js 15 App Router", not "the latest Next").
- **Concise.** If a sentence does not earn its place, cut it.

---

## 4. Vocabulary

**Use:** audience, readers, community, engineers, subscribers, technical authority, educational content, ship, build, deep dive, trade-off.

**Do not use:** client, customer, lead, sale, proposal, service delivery, agency, software house, technical partner, or any selling language. We do not sell services and we do not discuss selling.

**Jargon:** use it when it is the precise term, and define it on first use if it is not universal.

---

## 5. Formatting rules

- **No em dash character.** Use commas, colons, or periods.
- **Code:** fenced blocks with language tags; inline code for identifiers; keep examples runnable and minimal.
- **Headings:** sentence case, descriptive, ordered (one H1, then H2/H3).
- **Links:** descriptive anchor text; link to primary sources.
- **Lists:** for criteria, steps, and trade-offs; not as a crutch to avoid prose.
- **Bilingual:** en-US and pt-BR with full parity; both read natively, neither is a machine translation.

---

## 6. Anti-patterns (avoid)

- Generic, AI-sounding prose ("In today's fast-paced world…", "Let's dive in!", empty summaries).
- Hype and superlatives without evidence.
- Fear-based or clickbait framing.
- Padding to hit a length.
- Talking down to the reader or gatekeeping.
- Any selling or "customer" framing.

---

## 7. Quick voice test

Before publishing, ask:
- Would a senior engineer respect this, or roll their eyes?
- Could a competitor swap their name in and publish it unchanged? (If yes, it is too generic.)
- Is every claim backed, and every sentence earning its place?
- Does it sound like a human engineer, not a content generator?
