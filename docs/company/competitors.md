## Why this document exists

We do not compete for contracts; we compete for **engineers' attention and trust**. Our reference set is the brands whose engineering content developers actually respect, read, and share. This document maps who they are, what we admire, and what we borrow (and what we do differently), so we aim at the right bar.

Rule: we learn from these brands, we never copy them, and we never disparage anyone. We earn our place by being genuinely useful on our ecosystem.

---

## 1. The bar we measure against

These are engineering publications and technical brands with content engineers respect. For each: what we admire, and the lesson for us.

### Vercel (blog, changelog, docs)
- **Admire:** crisp writing on Next.js and the frontend platform, strong visuals, deep-but-readable posts.
- **Lesson:** own a topic (Next.js) with authority and clarity; pair depth with excellent presentation.

### PostHog (blog, handbook, "product for engineers")
- **Admire:** unmistakable voice, radical transparency, geeky and human at once, huge content output that still feels authentic.
- **Lesson:** a distinct voice and honesty beat polish; publish a lot without becoming generic.

### Supabase (blog, launch weeks, docs)
- **Admire:** developer-first storytelling, database and backend depth, community energy.
- **Lesson:** make deep backend topics exciting; build momentum with consistent, themed content.

### Trigger.dev (blog, technical deep dives)
- **Admire:** genuinely deep engineering write-ups, open build-in-public engineering culture.
- **Lesson:** deep dives into real problems earn respect; show the actual engineering.

### Railway (blog, changelog)
- **Admire:** clean infra/DevX content, approachable explanations of hard operational topics.
- **Lesson:** make infrastructure and deployment approachable without dumbing it down.

### Stripe (Stripe Engineering / press)
- **Admire:** rigor, clarity, and craft; API design and reliability writing that sets a standard.
- **Lesson:** rigor and clarity are a brand; treat writing as engineering.

### Cloudflare (engineering blog)
- **Admire:** deep systems and networking posts that are still readable; consistent technical authority.
- **Lesson:** you can go very deep and still keep a broad engineering audience if the writing is clear.

### GitHub (GitHub Engineering blog)
- **Admire:** real-world scale stories, honest post-mortems, credible practitioner voice.
- **Lesson:** lessons-learned and post-mortems are powerful, trustworthy formats.

### Node.js Blog
- **Admire:** authoritative, source-of-truth updates on the runtime we build on.
- **Lesson:** be the reliable, accurate reference for the parts of the stack we cover.

### React Blog
- **Admire:** careful, authoritative communication of changes to a huge audience.
- **Lesson:** clarity and precision when explaining changes builds long-term trust.

## 2. What we do differently

- **Focused ecosystem.** Most of these are company blogs about their own product. We are ecosystem-focused: end-to-end JS/TS (Next.js, NestJS, databases, CI/CD, testing), not tied to a single product.
- **Independent voice.** We can be honest about trade-offs across tools because we are not selling any of them.
- **Bilingual.** en-US and pt-BR parity widens our reach, especially in the Portuguese-speaking engineering community.

---

## 3. Where we are realistically behind

- **Scale and reach.** These are large, established brands. We start small and compound through consistency.
- **Back catalog.** They have years of content. We build ours one excellent piece at a time.
- **Distribution.** They have big audiences already. Our lever is LinkedIn growth plus genuinely shareable content (see [LinkedIn Growth Playbook](../marketing/linkedin-growth-playbook.md)).

Naming the gap keeps us honest and focused on the one thing we control: quality and consistency.

---

## 4. Maintaining this document

Review a few times a year. Add brands whose engineering content sets a bar worth chasing; drop ones that stop being relevant. Always capture the concrete lesson, not just the name.
