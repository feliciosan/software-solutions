## Why this document exists

Case studies are our strongest proof asset. A founder trusts "here is what we did for someone like you" more than any claim we make about ourselves. This document is the reusable template and the rules for writing one that is persuasive and completely honest. Honesty is not a constraint on the persuasion here; it is the source of it. A single invented metric would poison every real story we tell.

Until we have client results we can share with permission, the website uses clearly-labeled representative examples, not case studies. This template is what a real case study looks like when we have one.

---

## 1. Non-negotiable honesty rules

From [Values](../company/values.md) and [Brand Positioning](../company/brand-positioning.md):

- **Every claim is true and, ideally, verifiable.** No invented client names, revenue, user counts, or outcomes.
- **Get permission.** Client name, logo, quotes, and metrics are used only with explicit consent. If the client wants anonymity, anonymize honestly ("a fintech startup") rather than inventing a name.
- **Numbers are real or absent.** If we cannot cite a real metric, we describe the outcome qualitatively. We never estimate a number and present it as fact.
- **Prior-employer experience is not a CodaCrew case study.** Work our engineers did at Hugo Boss, Globo, and so on is framed as prior professional experience, never as a CodaCrew client engagement.
- **Attribution is proportional.** We describe our contribution accurately, without claiming sole credit for a team outcome.

If a story cannot be told truthfully and compellingly, we do not publish it.

## 2. The structure (why this shape works)

Case studies follow the problem-to-result arc because that is how a prospect evaluates fit: they see their own situation in the problem, and they see their desired future in the result. The four canonical sections (aligned with the website Case Studies framing and the original strategy) are: **Problem, Solution, Technologies, Result.** The template below expands them for a full write-up while keeping that spine.

---

## 3. The template

Copy everything below the line into a new case study and fill it in. Delete guidance in parentheses.

---

### [Client or descriptor]: [One-line outcome]

**Category:** (Startup / SaaS / AI / Modernization / Team Augmentation, from the [Service Catalog](../sales/service-catalog.md))
**Timeline:** (e.g. 10 weeks) · **Engagement:** (e.g. MVP Development)
**Stack:** (e.g. Next.js, Node.js, PostgreSQL, AWS)

**Summary (2 to 3 sentences).**
(The whole story in brief, for skimmers and for the card preview. Who the client was, what they needed, and the honest outcome.)

#### The problem
(What situation did the founder face? What was at risk? Frame it in the founder's terms: momentum, cost, scale, uncertainty. This is where a similar prospect recognizes themselves. Name the constraints honestly, including the ones that made it hard.)

#### What we did (the solution)
(Our approach and the key decisions. Emphasize judgment, not just technology: why this architecture, why this scope, what trade-offs we made and why. Show the "senior engineering plus product thinking" pillars in action. Keep it understandable to a non-technical founder.)

#### Technologies
(The stack and why it fit this problem. Tie choices to founder consequences: speed, scale, cost, maintainability. Not a résumé of buzzwords.)

#### The result
(What actually happened. Use real, permissioned metrics if we have them: time to launch, users, performance, cost saved, funding raised. If we do not have shareable numbers, describe the concrete qualitative outcome: shipped to production, validated with users, scaled without a rewrite. Never fabricate.)

> "Client quote, if given with permission." (Real quotes only. If none, omit the quote block entirely rather than inventing one.)

**What this shows.** (One line tying the story back to a positioning pillar: senior engineering, startup speed, scalable architecture, product thinking, or partnership.)

## 4. Writing guidance

- **Lead with the outcome,** then explain how we got there. Busy founders read the result first.
- **Make the prospect the hero.** The client is the protagonist; we are the guide who de-risked their path.
- **Show judgment, not just output.** The interesting part is the decision, not the framework.
- **Translate technical work into business value** (faster launch, no rewrite, lower cost, ready to scale).
- **Be specific.** "Scaled to handle a traffic spike during launch" beats "highly scalable solution."
- **Keep it honest and modest.** Real and specific is more persuasive than grand and vague.
- Follow the voice and house style in [Content Strategy](./content-strategy.md), including no em dash character and full `en-US` / `pt-BR` parity if published on the site.

---

## 5. Data to collect (ideally during and after the engagement)

Gather these while the work is fresh, so a future case study is easy and truthful:

- The founder's original problem and goal, in their words.
- Key decisions we made and the reasoning.
- The stack and why.
- Real metrics: launch date, timeline, users, performance, cost, funding, retention (whatever is honestly measurable).
- A permissioned quote.
- Explicit consent on what may be shared publicly (name, logo, numbers, quote).

Capture consent as part of [Client Onboarding](../sales/client-onboarding.md) and project wrap-up so we are never scrambling later.

---

## 7. Quality gate before publishing a case study

- [ ] Client consent obtained for every named/quoted/measured element.
- [ ] Every claim is true; every number is real and permissioned (or omitted).
- [ ] Our contribution is described accurately and proportionally.
- [ ] It ties clearly to at least one positioning pillar.
- [ ] Both locales if it goes on the site; house style respected (no em dash).
- [ ] A similar-fit prospect would recognize their own problem in it.

## Maintaining this document

Update the template as we learn what persuades in real sales conversations (which sections prospects react to, what proof moves them). Keep the honesty rules in Section 1 exactly as strict as they are. They are the reason the case studies are worth anything.
