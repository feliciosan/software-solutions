## Why this document exists

The proposal is where positioning meets money. A great proposal makes a founder feel understood and confident; a weak one turns us into interchangeable vendors compared on price. This document defines how we qualify, scope, price, and present, so that every proposal reinforces "senior technical partner" rather than "dev shop for hire." It is opinionated because the wrong proposal loses the right client, and the right proposal filters out the wrong one.

Governing principle, from [Brand Positioning](../company/brand-positioning.md): **we sell outcomes and partnership, never hours.** The proposal is an argument about the founder's future, not a quote for labor.

---

## 1. Qualify before you propose

A proposal is expensive to write and cheap to waste. Do not write one until the lead is qualified against the [Ideal Customer Profile](../company/ideal-customer-profile.md).

**Proceed when:**
- The founder or decision maker is in the conversation and can move.
- The need maps to a service in the [Service Catalog](./service-catalog.md).
- They value outcome and partnership over lowest price.
- There is enough clarity (or a discovery step) to scope responsibly.

**Redirect or decline when (red flags from the ICP):**
- "What is your hourly rate?" is the whole conversation.
- Lowest-bid comparison; price is the only axis.
- Refusal to be involved in decisions.
- Guarantees demanded with no discovery.
- Out of ICP (simple site, one-page landing, committee procurement).

A fast, kind "no" protects the pipeline and the team. See objection framing in [Competitors](../company/competitors.md).

## 2. Discover before you scope

Never scope in the dark. Before a fixed proposal, run a discovery conversation (or a paid discovery engagement for larger work) to establish:

- The real problem and the business goal behind it.
- Who the users are and what "validated" looks like.
- Must-have vs nice-to-have (push hard to cut to the core).
- Constraints: timeline, budget band, existing systems, team.
- What success means to the founder, in their words.

The website contact form already captures a budget band and project context; use it as the starting point, not the end.

---

## 3. Scope honestly

- **Cut to the core.** Recommend the smaller, sharper scope that validates the idea. Talking a founder into more than they need is off-mission (see [Values](../company/values.md)).
- **Make inclusions explicit.** State that tests, code review, CI/CD, and documentation are included; they are how we work, not line-item upsells (see [Engineering Standards](../engineering/engineering-standards.md)).
- **Make exclusions explicit.** Name what is out of scope to prevent scope creep and protect the relationship.
- **State assumptions.** Every estimate rests on assumptions; write them down so changes are conversations, not conflicts.

---

## 4. Pricing posture

- **Value-based, never hourly.** Price the outcome and the engagement. Presenting an hourly rate invites commoditization (see [Competitors](../company/competitors.md)).
- **Give ranges honestly** where uncertainty is real, and reduce the range through discovery rather than false precision.
- **Never discount to win a price shopper.** If budget is the obstacle, cut scope, not quality or rate. A cheaper CodaCrew is a contradiction.
- **Anchor on cost of the alternative:** a cheap build that needs a rewrite, months lost to hiring, momentum lost to a slow vendor. Make the true cost of "cheaper" visible.
- **Structure for trust:** milestone-based payments tied to demos and deliverables, so the founder sees value before the next payment.

Specific numbers are a live commercial decision made per deal by the founders; this document sets the posture, not a rate card.

## 5. Anatomy of a CodaCrew proposal

Keep it tight, founder-facing, and outcome-led. A good proposal is short.

1. **Their goal, in their words.** Prove we listened. Restate the problem and the desired outcome. This is the most important section.
2. **Our understanding and approach.** How we would tackle it, and the key decisions, in plain language. Show product thinking, not a feature dump.
3. **Scope.** What is included, explicitly, and what is not. Phased if appropriate (for example Discovery, then MVP).
4. **Timeline.** Realistic, sprint-based, with demo checkpoints.
5. **Investment.** The value-based price or range, structured by milestone. Framed as investment and outcome.
6. **What is included as standard.** The quality baseline and the partnership (weekly demos, direct access, ownership of code).
7. **Why CodaCrew.** A brief, honest differentiator tied to the pillars, plus relevant proof (prior experience, case studies where available; see [Case Study Template](../marketing/case-study-template.md)).
8. **Next steps.** One clear, low-friction action.

## 6. Voice and honesty in proposals

- Same voice as everywhere: direct, confident, founder-framed, no jargon for its own sake. See [Content Strategy](../marketing/content-strategy.md).
- **No em dash character.** Commas, colons, periods, or parentheses.
- **No invented proof.** No fabricated metrics, client names, or outcomes. Prior experience is framed as prior experience. See [Brand Positioning](../company/brand-positioning.md).
- **No overpromising.** Do not promise dates or outcomes we cannot stand behind. Under-promise on certainty, over-deliver on quality.
- Bilingual as needed: deliver in the client's language, at full quality.

---

## 7. Objection handling in the proposal stage

Address the likely objection before it is raised. Full talk tracks in [Competitors](../company/competitors.md). Common ones:

- **"It is cheaper elsewhere."** Reframe from rate to risk: who makes the architecture decisions, and what a rebuild costs when the product takes off.
- **"Can you go faster/cheaper?"** Offer a smaller scope, never a lower quality bar.
- **"Why not just hire?"** Speed to a validated product now, with the option to build or augment the team later.

---

## 8. After sending

- **Follow up** with a clear, respectful cadence. A proposal is the start of a conversation, not a verdict.
- **Log the outcome** and the reason (won, lost, why). Win/loss reasons are the best input to [Competitors](../company/competitors.md) and the [ICP](../company/ideal-customer-profile.md).
- **On acceptance,** move straight into [Client Onboarding](./client-onboarding.md) so momentum is not lost.

---

## 9. Anti-patterns

- Sending a proposal before qualifying or discovering.
- Leading with price or an hourly rate.
- A generic proposal that ignores the founder's specific goal.
- Padding scope to raise the number.
- Discounting to win, and recategorizing ourselves as a commodity.
- Vague scope with no stated exclusions or assumptions (the seed of scope creep).
- Any invented proof.
